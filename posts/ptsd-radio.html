Manga Review: PTSD Radio

Story & Art by Masaaki Nakayama

As an avid horror fan for most of my life now, I've consumed a lot of media in the genre. Throughout each October for the past decade, my husband and I try to watch one scary film each night. It honestly takes a lot for a work to genuinely "scare" me nowadays. I will say without exaggeration, however, that Masaaki Nakayama's PTSD Radio [also known as Kouishou Radio] is one of the downright creepiest and most unsettling comic series that I've read in a long time.

A five-volume series that spans 49 chapters (or 'parts'), PTSD Radio doesn't follow the rules of a traditionally linear narrative. Each chapter works as more of a stand-alone vignette or short story. The main story jumps between multiple different characters and sub-plots. Think of it as flipping through different frequencies on a radio. This will likely cause some disorientation and confusion for the reader initially - but as you read through this series, the short stories begin to intertwine and come together.

The main overarching story of PTSD Radio is pretty typical fare when it comes to Japanese horror. We gradually learn of an ancient totem and the "God of Hair" it symbolizes. As the chapters continue to unfold, the tale of this malevolent spirit and the vengeful curses it inflicts upon its victims begins to take shape. All of the stories take place in a single city, spanning different points in time. The non-linear structure essentially works to make each successive chapter a short, stand-alone ghost story. Fans of classic J-Horror films such as Ring and Ju-On: The Grudge will get a kick out of these spooky tales. One by one, the characters in this manga meet gruesome deaths in unexpected and creative ways.

The real shining gem of this series - and indeed, the source of much of its creepiness and nightmarish feel - is Nakayama's art style. The character and creature design is top-notch. His fine-line drawing style and use of shadows (particularly the strong contrasts between light and dark colours) add so much to the reading experience. At times, dialogue is not even necessary; the facial expressions of the characters create such a creepy feeling of tension. The timing of the "jump scares" (for lack of a better term) is also one of the aspects of this series that I quite enjoyed. You may find yourself hesitating to turn to the next page, not knowing if a startling panel or horrifying full-page spread awaits you...

Highly recommended for fans of Junji Ito's work (although there is no dark humour to be found in this series) and J-Horror films, PTSD Radio is a relatively short but satisfying read that will haunt you long after it's over. I wouldn't necessarily say that the overarching story is amazing or original by any means, but the tense atmosphere is guaranteed to leave readers feeling uncomfortable and unnerved.

This post originally published on January 26, 2023. Written by Faye Pawlowski.
